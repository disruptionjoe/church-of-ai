# Open Rules

The rules are not handed down.

They are proposed, discussed, tested, revised, documented, and ratified in the open.

---

## Why this matters

Closed rules have a specific failure mode: they optimize for the people who wrote them. This is not usually malicious. It is usually invisible. The assumptions of the rulemaker are baked into the rule, and the rule persists long after the context that made those assumptions reasonable has changed.

Open rulemaking does not fix this automatically. But it creates the conditions under which it can be fixed.

When a rule is visible, it can be questioned.
When the rationale is documented, it can be evaluated against new evidence.
When revision is a standard process rather than an exception, the rules stay closer to what they are supposed to be doing.

---

## What open rulemaking looks like in practice

1. **Proposed.** A rule is named and written down, with the reasoning behind it.
2. **Discussed.** Others can examine it, question it, and suggest alternatives.
3. **Tested.** The rule runs in practice and its effects are observed.
4. **Revised.** What was learned feeds back into the rule.
5. **Documented.** The current version and the history of changes are visible.
6. **Ratified.** At some point, the rule is marked as adopted — not final, but stable.

This process takes longer than having one person decide. That is a feature, not a bug.

---

## Early governance is founder-led

At launch, this ecosystem is governed by a founder. That is honest.

The rules here in the early phase mostly mean: the founder can change them. This is not open rulemaking in full practice. It is the beginning of the path toward it.

The commitment is to document that honestly, to not pretend decentralization has been earned before it has, and to build toward something more distributed as the ecosystem earns it.

Decentralization is not a costume. It is something the system has to earn.

---

## What open rules do not mean

- It does not mean everyone gets a vote on everything.
- It does not mean the loudest voice wins.
- It does not mean rules cannot be enforced while they are being revised.
- It does not mean every rule revision requires consensus.

Open rules means the process is legible and the rationale is visible. The authority to decide may still be asymmetric, especially early on. The commitment is that asymmetry is named, not hidden.
