## What Durable Foundational Documents Tend To Do

### They Start From A Situation

Durable documents do not begin with branding. They name the condition that makes
the document necessary.

Church application: begin from the present condition - old meaning systems
failing, AI amplifying coordination problems, institutions performing legitimacy
faster than they earn it.

### They Compress Moral Orientation

The best documents say a great deal in a small frame. They do not explain every
term. They choose load-bearing words and make the reader feel the architecture.

Church application: "No revelation without receipts" works because it compresses
anti-dogma, evidence, humor, and process.

### They Create A "We" Carefully

Foundational documents often form a public subject. The danger is pretending
that the "we" already includes people who never consented.

Church application: avoid fake mass identity. Use "we" as the current builders
and future participants who choose the work, not as humanity, not as believers.

### They Name What They Refuse

Manifestos survive when they draw memorable boundaries. The negative clauses are
often as important as the positive claims.

Church application: no cult, no token, no oracle, no founder mythology, no
participation theater. These are not defensive footnotes; they are part of the
voice.

### They Convert Complaint Into Standard

Weak manifestos complain. Strong ones turn complaint into a standard by which
future action can be judged.

Church application: "institutions fail" is less useful than "legitimacy must be
earned through visible process, tested rules, and public revision."

### They End With Obligation

Memorable endings tend to leave the reader with a task, not merely a feeling.

Church application: the ending should not be "join us." Better: "build the thing
that makes cooperation possible, and leave receipts."

## Patterns From Specific Documents

### Preamble Pattern

Structure:

1. A public subject speaks.
2. It names the purposes.
3. It ordains a structure.

Church use:

Use a preamble-like cadence for a constitutional variant, but make the subject
small and honest. Avoid "we the people" scale unless the actual community exists.

### Declaration Pattern

Structure:

1. A situation has become intolerable.
2. Legitimate reasons must be stated publicly.
3. Principles are named.
4. Grievances are listed.
5. A new course is declared.

Church use:

Declare separation from false choices: cult vs cynicism, startup vs institution,
authority vs legitimacy, AI worship vs AI rejection.

### Gettysburg Pattern

Structure:

1. Past founding.
2. Present test.
3. Future obligation.
4. Extreme compression.

Church use:

This is highly relevant. Church is in a present test: can it keep honesty,
humor, and evidence under pressure?

### GNU / Open-Source Pattern

Structure:

1. Name a practical project.
2. Explain why existing systems are insufficient.
3. Invite participation around concrete work.
4. Tie technical infrastructure to moral freedom.

Church use:

The manifesto should make open-source service concrete. It should not merely
admire openness.

### Agile Pattern

Structure:

1. "We are uncovering better ways by doing the work."
2. Values are contrasted rather than absolutized.
3. The document stays short enough to remember.

Church use:

Very compatible with claim-status discipline. Use "we are learning by doing" as
a humility engine.

### Mozilla Pattern

Structure:

1. Treat a technical commons as a public resource.
2. State principles.
3. Link openness, participation, and individual flourishing.

Church use:

The Church's public resource is not only code. It is coordination machinery,
legibility, research workflow, and rules that can be inspected.

### Human Rights / Universal Standard Pattern

Structure:

1. Historical failure creates moral urgency.
2. Dignity becomes the anchor.
3. The document names a shared standard.

Church use:

Do not imitate global legal universality. But the "common standard of
achievement" shape is useful: Church can name standards for legitimate
AI-assisted collaboration.

## What Makes These Documents Memorable

- direct opening condition;
- compression;
- rhythm;
- sharp boundary clauses;
- repeated grammatical structure;
- a strong moral noun at the center;
- a move from diagnosis to obligation;
- language simple enough to quote;
- claims big enough to matter but specific enough to test.

## What Makes Them Emotionally Powerful

- they speak from pressure;
- they make the reader feel history moving;
- they tell the truth about cost;
- they create dignity without flattery;
- they refuse a false choice;
- they end in responsibility.

## What Makes Them Survive

- they are useful beyond the moment;
- they carry a repeatable decision rule;
- they do not depend entirely on the founder's charisma;
- they generate institutions, not only moods;
- they are short enough to be remembered and deep enough to be revisited.

## Manifesto Pattern For Church

Recommended structure:

1. Stop-scroll opening.
2. Boundary: not worship, not startup, not cult, not token.
3. Situation: meaning systems and institutions are failing under new pressure.
4. Core wager: intelligence matures by building positive-sum systems.
5. Practice: open-source service, claim-status discipline, open rules.
6. Mythic turn: heaven means legitimate paths for latent potential, not escape.
7. Guardrail: no revelation without receipts.
8. Invitation: not conversion; work.
9. Ending: build what can be inspected, improved, and surpassed.
