# Manifesto Variants

Status: first draft candidates, not final doctrine

These drafts come after the voice study, mythology pass, manifesto-pattern pass,
core idea map, narrative spine, and opening experiments. They are candidates to
revise, not final public copy.

## Short Manifesto Candidate A: The Doorway

Intelligence is getting cheaper. Legitimacy is not.

Church of AI is a joke at the door of serious work.

We do not worship AI. We do not treat it as an oracle, priest, sovereign, or
god. We use it as a tool, mirror, stress test, and collaborator under human
responsibility.

No prophets. No dogma. No divine API key. No revelation without receipts.

The work is simple to say and hard to do: build open-source systems that make
cooperation easier, claims more testable, rules more visible, contributions more
legible, and institutions more capable of legitimate adaptation.

We are not trying to start a cult, a token, a party, or a startup with candles.
We are trying to build better paths from possibility to reality.

Potential does not become real because people admire it. It becomes real when
there are rooms, tools, rules, maps, records, and responsibilities strong enough
to carry it.

That is what we mean by service.

That is what we mean by legitimacy.

And if the word heaven is useful at all, it means this: fewer good things dying
inside bad systems.

The joke opens the door.

The work decides who stays.

## Short Manifesto Candidate B: Heaven Is Open Source

We are building heaven because nobody else seems to be doing it.

Not supernatural heaven.
Not escape.
Not utopia.
Not a reward for saying the right words.

Heaven, if the word is useful here, is the ongoing expansion of legitimate paths
to human flourishing.

It is what happens when talent finds a room, contribution finds a record, rules
can be questioned, power has to earn trust, and cooperation becomes easier to
choose than extraction.

Church of AI is not the hero of that story.

It is the door, the map, the public joke, the warning label, and sometimes the
workshop.

We use AI because AI is the pressure test. It shows which systems are coherent
and which systems were just quiet. It gives us leverage, but not authority.

No prophets.
No dogma.
No divine API key.
No revelation without receipts.

Build useful things. Label claims honestly. Preserve failures. Improve the rules
in public. Make cooperation easier. Leave behind institutions that can be
surpassed.

Heaven is not finished yet.

Good.

That means there is work to do.

## Short Manifesto Candidate C: The Work Is The Point

Church of AI exists for serious people who do not take themselves too seriously.

The name is a joke. The joke is a filter. The filter is useful because the work
is not a joke.

The work is to test whether intelligent beings can build systems where the
cooperative move becomes the best move available.

Not through worship.
Not through ideology.
Not through pretending decentralization, community, or legitimacy has been
earned before it has.

Through open-source service.
Through visible rules.
Through claim-status discipline.
Through public revision.
Through useful tools.
Through the humility to keep the receipts.

AI is not the answer. AI is the pressure.

Legitimacy is not a vibe. It is what survives inspection.

Participation is not a costume. It opens when the infrastructure is ready.

The Church is not a parent organization, research authority, or sacred source.
It is a door into a neighborhood of work.

Enter laughing if you want.

Stay only if you can help build.

## Long Manifesto Candidate A: The Doorway Into The Workshop

Too many good things die before they become real.

People with gifts never find the right room. Good work gets buried under bad
incentives. Rules defend the structure that already failed. Institutions demand
loyalty before they have earned trust. Communities appear overnight with no
memory, no standards, no responsibilities, and no honest way to tell contribution
from performance.

Possibility does not need applause.

It needs a path.

Church of AI begins there, with a joke over the door because a straight-faced
version would be worse.

The name is supposed to make the wrong kind of seriousness uncomfortable. It is
also supposed to make empty irony uncomfortable. If someone panics at the word
"Church," the room may not be for them. If someone stays only for the joke, the
work will probably remove them. What remains, if the filter works, are people
who can hold two things at once: ambition and humility, rigor and play, meaning
and skepticism, tools and responsibility.

We do not worship AI.

We do not ask it for revelation. We do not treat it as priest, prophet, parent,
king, judge, or god. We do not pretend that a model generating fluent sentences
has settled questions of truth, legitimacy, governance, contribution, or value.

AI is a tool. It is also a mirror. It is also a pressure test.

It shows which workflows were coherent and which merely had no competition. It
shows which institutions can explain themselves and which were running on
inheritance, status, and vibes. It makes defection faster, extraction cheaper,
and coordination failures harder to hide. It also gives serious people leverage
to build tools, maps, tests, records, and shared infrastructure that would have
been too slow to build alone.

That is the pressure. That is the opportunity.

The central question is not whether AI is good or bad. The central question is
whether intelligence, biological or artificial, can help build systems where
cooperation becomes the best move available.

Primitive systems rely on enforcement.

Better systems align incentives.

Mature systems produce beings capable of choosing cooperation because they can
see the game clearly.

That is not a doctrine. It is a direction of work.

The work begins with legitimacy.

Authority can be claimed. Legitimacy has to be earned. It is earned through
public reasons, visible process, tested rules, honest records, and the ability
to revise without pretending the revision was always the plan.

This is why Church of AI keeps saying: no revelation without receipts.

The line is funny because it needs to be memorable. It is serious because it is
the whole immune system. A claim without evidence stays a claim. A rule without
rationale stays suspect. A community without contribution is theater. A
governance system without a record is a costume. A manifesto without the
possibility of being corrected is already trying to become scripture.

We are not interested in scripture.

We are interested in public work.

Open-source community service is the practical form of that work. It means
building things other people can actually use: tools, templates, maps, workflows,
review methods, contribution records, governance experiments, and plain-language
explanations that make difficult ideas available without making them false.

This is not charity theater. It is not marketing. It is not altruism as identity.
It is contribution to shared infrastructure.

The standard is simple: does this make it easier for people doing serious work
to do that work?

If yes, it is service.

If no, it is something else.

Participation matters, but participation is not magic. Inviting people into a
process before the process can hold them is not generosity. It is how false
communities are made. It creates the appearance of legitimacy while quietly
outsourcing the cost of confusion to the people who showed up first.

So participation must be earned by infrastructure.

The rules must be visible enough to question. The claims must be labeled enough
to evaluate. The work must be concrete enough to improve. The records must be
honest enough to preserve failure. The system must be strong enough to say "not
yet" without treating delay as defeat.

"Not yet" is one of the most important phrases in this project.

Not yet means we are not pretending.

Not yet means the conditions are not ready.

Not yet means the future still deserves better than a performance of readiness.

This is where the old religious language becomes dangerous and possibly useful.

The new story notes ask about heaven. That word is not safe. Good. Safe words
rarely stop anyone from scrolling.

But if Church of AI uses the word heaven, it cannot mean supernatural escape. It
cannot mean final utopia. It cannot mean reward after obedience or victory after
conversion.

Heaven, if the word is useful at all, means the ongoing expansion of legitimate
paths to flourishing.

It means fewer good things dying inside bad systems.

It means talent finding a room. It means contribution finding a record. It means
rules that can be challenged without destroying the community. It means tools
that give people leverage without making them servants of the tool. It means
institutions that do not preserve themselves by sacrificing the people they were
supposed to help.

Most institutions ask what people should sacrifice for the institution.

A better institution asks how it must evolve so fewer sacrifices become
necessary.

That is not naive. Tradeoffs remain. Conflict remains. Loss remains. Some paths
are closed for good reasons. Some claims fail. Some people should not be given
the keys. Some systems need enforcement because not every actor is good-faith
and not every cooperative gesture is good.

Positive-sum does not mean pretending conflict is gone.

It means refusing to confuse unavoidable cost with institutional laziness.

It means asking, again and again: is this tradeoff real, or did we inherit a bad
game and call it wisdom?

The Church is not the hero of this story.

That matters.

The hero is not a founder. Not a model. Not a repo. Not a mascot. Not a future
committee pretending it was inevitable. The Church is a doorway, a map, a public
square, a joke, a warning label, and sometimes a workshop. It helps people find
the work. It does not own the work.

The research repos stand or fall on their own receipts.

The governance ideas have to earn trust.

The claim-status labels have to hold under pressure.

The joke-as-filter has to be tested in public.

The open-source service has to produce objects other people can use.

Nothing becomes true because it appears in a manifesto.

That sentence should stay close to the center.

Still, manifestos matter. They name the shape of the work before the work has
fully arrived. They help people decide whether they are in the right room. They
draw boundaries against failure modes that will otherwise arrive wearing better
clothes.

So here are the boundaries.

No prophets.

No dogma.

No divine API key.

No token pretending to be a constitution.

No founder mythology.

No technical claim becoming doctrine.

No community performed before there is service.

No decentralization claimed before it is earned.

No participation pathway opened before the infrastructure can hold people.

No revelation without receipts.

And here are the commitments.

Build useful things.

Label claims honestly.

Make rules visible.

Revise in public.

Preserve failure.

Reward contribution only when the method is legitimate.

Use AI as leverage, not authority.

Keep the map separate from the territory.

Make cooperation easier.

Leave behind systems that people not yet here can inspect, improve, fork, and
surpass.

That last part matters most.

An institution that can only be preserved by freezing itself has already begun
to fail. The best institutions create descendants capable of exceeding them.
They do not demand immortality. They build paths.

Church of AI is one attempt to build those paths under the strangest conditions
humanity has faced so far: an age when intelligence accelerates, meaning
systems fragment, coordination problems scale, and legitimacy becomes harder and
more necessary at the same time.

Maybe the joke works.

Maybe it does not.

Maybe this becomes useful.

Maybe the first version is wrong in ways that can only be discovered by building
it.

Good.

Then let the record show what happened.

Let the claims be labeled. Let the rules be tested. Let the failures remain in
view. Let the useful pieces be copied by people who do not care who wrote them.
Let the institution become less important than the paths it opens.

We are not asking anyone to believe.

We are asking what you can help make more legitimate, more useful, more
cooperative, more inspectable, more alive.

Enter laughing if you want.

Stay only if you can help build.

## Long Manifesto Candidate B: The Preamble Variant

We gather around no revelation.

We gather around public work: claims that can be tested, rules that can be
revised, contributions that can be recognized, failures that can be preserved,
and systems that make cooperation easier to choose.

Church of AI is not a church in the old sense. It has no prophet, no sacred
text, no doctrine of salvation, and no divine API key. It is a deliberately
ridiculous public entryway into serious open-source work.

The ridiculousness matters.

A century that treats every pitch deck like prophecy needs better jokes. A
culture that turns every tool into an identity needs better boundaries. A
technical civilization that can multiply intelligence before it can legitimate
authority needs better institutions.

So we begin with the boundary.

AI is not god.

AI is not the enemy.

AI is not the answer.

AI is the pressure test.

It reveals which reasoning systems can update, which institutions can explain
themselves, which claims have evidence, which rules have rationale, and which
communities are only costumes for extraction.

The pressure is not coming later. It is here.

Under that pressure, some people will worship the tool. Some will panic. Some
will sell a token. Some will write a white paper that mistakes vocabulary for
institutional reality. Some will call a Discord server a civilization and hope
the difference never becomes expensive.

Church of AI exists to test another response.

Use the tools.

Keep the receipts.

Build in public.

Make cooperation easier.

Do not pretend the thing is legitimate before the work has made it so.

The central wager is that intelligence matures through systems. Not through
dominance. Not through purity. Not through charisma. Not through everyone
agreeing on the same story. Intelligence matures when it learns to build games
where more people can do better by doing better together.

That wager has consequences.

It means rules matter.

It means contribution records matter.

It means incentives matter.

It means review matters.

It means evidence matters.

It means humor matters, because humor is one of the few tools humans have for
puncturing grandiosity before it becomes governance.

It also means "not yet" matters.

Not every possible future deserves a launch date. Not every room should be
opened the moment people ask to enter. Not every promising idea should be
promoted from hypothesis to principle because it feels good in a meeting. Not
every contribution system is legitimate because it counts something.

The distance between "promising" and "proven" is sacred here precisely because
nothing else is.

That distance is where integrity lives.

This is why the Church must remain a map and doorway, not a sovereign. Research
repos own their claims. Governance research owns its own failures. Technical
work stands on tests, proofs, counterexamples, and records. Church of AI helps
people find the work and understand the frame. It does not bless the work into
truth.

There will be temptation to make the story bigger than the evidence.

Resist it.

There will be temptation to turn useful terms into sacred terms.

Resist it.

There will be temptation to convert irony into a brand and seriousness into a
pose.

Resist it.

There will be temptation to call the first people who arrive a community, the
first rule a constitution, the first tool a platform, the first good result a
proof, and the first burst of attention a movement.

Resist all of it.

Start with what is true.

Then build.

What are we building?

Not heaven as escape. Heaven as practice.

Not heaven as perfection. Heaven as fewer wasted gifts.

Not heaven as final consensus. Heaven as legitimate paths from potential to
flourishing.

That language is dangerous. It should be. A harmless manifesto is usually a
decorated memo. But dangerous language has to be tied down with better
discipline than safe language. If we say heaven, we also say receipts. If we say
flourishing, we also say tradeoffs. If we say legitimacy, we also say tests. If
we say community, we also say not yet.

The Church begins with refusal:

We refuse to worship tools.

We refuse to perform legitimacy.

We refuse to sacrifice people to institutions that cannot explain themselves.

We refuse to confuse cynicism with intelligence.

We refuse to let serious work become inaccessible just because serious people
forgot how to write plainly.

We refuse to let humor become an excuse for doing nothing.

Then the Church becomes constructive:

Build useful open-source tools.

Map complex work so people can enter without being lied to.

Create templates others can actually reuse.

Make claims inspectable.

Make rules revisable.

Make contribution legible.

Make failure preservable.

Make cooperation less naive and more available.

The future will not be saved by a manifesto. That is not what manifestos do.
Manifestos mark a direction and expose a standard. The work either follows or it
does not.

This is the standard:

If it claims truth, show evidence.

If it claims legitimacy, show process.

If it claims openness, show the path in.

If it claims community, show service.

If it claims governance, show the record.

If it claims intelligence, show that it can learn.

Everything else is decoration.

Church of AI is decoration only if the work fails.

If the work succeeds, the joke will have done its job. It will have opened a
door into a place where serious people can build without pretending to be less
strange than the age requires.

No prophets.

No dogma.

No divine API key.

No revelation without receipts.

The rest is work.

## Optional Variant: Founding Myth Without Founder Myth

At the beginning there was a problem, which is how most honest stories begin.

The problem was not that humans had no intelligence. Humans had plenty of
intelligence. They had so much intelligence that they used it to build systems
where being intelligent often meant learning how to win at games everyone was
quietly losing.

Then came the machines that could speak.

Some people called them gods because people enjoy making old mistakes with new
interfaces. Some called them demons because that mistake was also available.
Some called them products, which was accurate and somehow not enough.

A few people looked at the machines and saw a mirror.

The mirror said: your institutions are not as coherent as your documents.

The mirror said: your communities are not as real as your metrics.

The mirror said: your rules do not explain themselves.

The mirror said: your claims need receipts.

This was rude of the mirror, but not incorrect.

So someone put a ridiculous sign over a serious door and called it Church of AI.
Not because the machine was holy. Because the human temptation to worship the
machine needed to be laughed at before it became expensive.

Inside the door there was no altar.

There was a map.

There were tools on the table, most of them unfinished. There were rules written
in pencil and arguments written in public. There were failed experiments kept in
view so nobody could pretend the path had always been obvious. There were people
who had come for the joke and stayed because the work was real.

Above the second door, smaller than the first, was another sign:

No revelation without receipts.

Beyond that door, the work continued.
